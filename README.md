The Championship Cube: A Front Office Framework for Building NBA Champions 
Submitted to the MIT Sloan Sports Analytics Conference 2027

I started this project because I got tired of hearing championships explained as if talent alone was the answer. The Championship Cube is my attempt to break down what actually separates NBA champions from teams that look good on paper but never quite get there.

Instead of treating winning as one big, vague idea, I look at it through six connected faces: the roster, the players, the coach, the front office, the controllable external factors, and the uncontrollable ones. Each face answers a different question about how a champion is built, and the point is to show how those pieces interact rather than stand alone.

This is a research project built around NBA title teams and Finals runners-up from 1990 to 2026, using Basketball Reference as the main data source. I’m using Python, spreadsheets, and statistical analysis to turn a basketball argument into something measurable, testable, and hopefully useful.

The Six Faces
Team Face — What the roster looked like, how it was built, and how it performed in its era.

Player Face — Which players drove value, how they were acquired, and whether they fit their role.

Coach Face — How the coach built the system, set the tone, and matched the roster.

Front Office Face — How the team drafted, traded, signed, and shaped the long-term plan.

Controllable External Face — Things like load management, scheduling, and sports science.

Uncontrollable External Face — Injuries, randomness, and how well a team handled bad breaks.

What’s in the repo
data — Master champion dataset, era-adjusted tables, Finals runner-up comparisons, and coach analysis.

notes — Research notes, methodology, and working ideas.

scripts — Python scripts for era analysis, gap analysis, and logistic regression.

abstract — The abstract submitted to MIT Sloan Sports Analytics Conference 2027.

How I’m building it
Phase 1 — Data Collection: Building the full dataset of champions and runners-up across eras.

Phase 2 — Analysis: Looking for repeatable patterns and turning them into a scoring framework.

Phase 3 — Writing: Drafting the paper and organizing the six faces into a clear argument.

Phase 4 — Refinement: Revising, testing the logic, and preparing the final submission.
